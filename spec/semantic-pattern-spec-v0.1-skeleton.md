# Semantic Pattern Specification

**Version 0.1 — skeleton**
**Bodkin, July 2026**
**Status: working draft for internal research. Nothing here is final; everything here is testable**

---

## 0. One-paragraph summary

This specification defines a **meta-grammar**: the format in which any brand can declare its visual pattern language so that (a) designers can generate identity surfaces from it, (b) machines can recognise those surfaces from fragments by open, reproducible measurement, and (c) recognition resolves through the brand's own domain to a signed, versioned semantic identity. The spec defines the slots; each brand supplies the values. One format, infinite identities — the tartan register rebuilt on the web's trust rails

---

## 1. Purpose and scope

**In scope:** the grammar sheet format · signature feature definitions and their measurement · the signature locus declaration · distinctiveness and distance requirements · the two enrolment paths · the family/variation model · the recognition protocol and confidence reporting · the resolution and trust chain

**Out of scope:** payload encoding of any kind (this system identifies; the web resolves) · anti-counterfeiting of physical goods (a material and serialisation problem layered separately) · aesthetic judgement (the spec constrains measurability, never taste)

---

## 2. Terminology

**Grammar** — a brand's declared pattern language: alphabet, rules, rhythms, colour system, variation model
**Grammar sheet** — the published, machine-readable declaration of a grammar
**Surface** — any artefact generated by or conforming to a grammar
**Fragment** — any partial view of a surface (crop, photograph, damaged sample)
**Signature feature** — a measurable property of surfaces used for identification
**Signature locus** — the declared subset of features that carry an identity's identification load
**Canonical peak** — a feature value so common (tool default, geometric convention, crowd habit) that it carries no identification weight
**Dialect** — a permitted variation of a grammar (person, product, campaign, era)
**Enrolment** — publishing a grammar sheet at a brand's domain
**Recognition** — measuring a fragment's features against enrolled grammar sheets
**Resolution** — retrieving the signed semantic identity from the domain of a recognised grammar

## 3. The meta-grammar: slots every grammar declares

Every grammar sheet MUST declare:

### 3.1 Ground
The substrate assumption (e.g. white, specified stock, dark ground) that colour and contrast measurements are relative to

### 3.2 Structure
The organising system: grid (module, scales), field (density model), radial, band, or free-topology. Declared with its governing dimensions in relative units — ratios and module-relative sizes, never absolute pixels or millimetres

### 3.3 Primitive alphabet
The closed set of forms the grammar speaks with, each defined in module-relative or topology-relative terms. Alphabets SHOULD be small (guide: 3–9 primitives)

### 3.4 Combination rules
How primitives connect, cluster, repeat, offset and breathe: adjacency rules, permitted angles, spacing ratios, phase rules, rhythm constants

### 3.5 Colour system
The ink set (exact values in a stated colour space), the ground, the layering rule (e.g. multiply overprint), and — for print-destined grammars — the committed **relationships** (orderings, ratios, overlap consistencies) that survive lighting and reproduction where absolute values do not

### 3.6 Variation model
The declared dialect space: which parameters instances may vary, over what ranges, and what remains invariant. Defines family members (people, products), states (campaigns) and generations (eras) as measurable regions of parameter space

### 3.7 Signature locus — REQUIRED
The declaration of *where this identity's identification load lives*: the subset of features, with weights, that the recogniser scores. Rules:

- Features at canonical peaks are **excluded** from the locus regardless of measurement precision. Non-exhaustive peak examples: 0°/45°/90° orientations · ratios of 2.0, 1.5, φ · duty cycles of 1/2 and 1/3 · square grids per se · multiply blend per se
- Tool defaults are canonical peaks by definition. Chosen-with-love and defaulted-by-software are indistinguishable to measurement and are treated identically: discounted
- The locus MUST contain enough discounted-weight-bearing features that the conjunction meets the distance requirement in §5

*Rationale: audits 001 and 002 (companion documents) found that different real grammars keep their signatures in different dimensions — 001's in its ratio system, 002's in its ink set and primitive proportions. The spec therefore requires each identity to declare its locus rather than assuming one universal signature dimension*

## 4. The grammar sheet

Published at:

```text
https://{brand-domain}/.well-known/pattern-grammar
```

Format: JSON-LD (canonical) with a human-readable mirror. MUST include: the declarations of §3 · spec version · grammar version · issuer · signature reference (key published at the same domain, DID:web style) · pointer to the semantic identity endpoint

The grammar sheet is public by design. The khipu rule: **a grammar whose decoder lives in a few heads, or one company's model, is a dead language waiting to happen.** Openness of the sheet and the recogniser is a conformance requirement, not a preference

---

## 5. Distinctiveness and distance

- Grammar sheets define points in a shared measurable feature space
- Enrolment SHOULD verify a **minimum distance** from previously enrolled grammars, computed over signature-locus features only (peaks contribute zero)
- Implementations MUST test candidate grammars against a **control corpus** of non-enrolled pattern work; the false-positive rate against the corpus is a published property of the grammar, not a private one
- Distance computation is defined by this spec so that clearance is reproducible: the same two sheets yield the same distance for any implementer

*Open question, recorded honestly: the registry model here is decentralised (each domain publishes its own sheet), so "previously enrolled" is discoverable rather than centrally listed. Discovery mechanics are deferred to a later draft*

## 6. Enrolment paths

### 6.1 Generate (new identities)
Author the grammar inside the meta-grammar from day one. Benefits: evenly distributed signature (the genome in every cell), designed variation space, locus chosen deliberately off-peak

### 6.2 Enrol (existing identities): audit → commit → tune
1. **Audit** — measure the implicit rules out of existing work (method demonstrated in audits 001–002)
2. **Commit** — turn approximate intentions into exact declared values
3. **Tune** — where committed values sit on canonical peaks, apply sub-perceptual adjustments to relocate the locus into ownable space; where tuning would cost the identity's character (002's deliberate pixel-grid canon), shift the locus to dimensions the identity already owns (ink set, proportions) and state the dependence

Enrolment is the brand owner's act. Auditing a public identity demonstrates method; only the owner can commit, sign and publish

---

## 7. Family and variation

- **Instance** (person, product): a declared sub-region of parameter space; distinguishable by measurement from sibling instances. Honest cardinality note: this mechanism comfortably distinguishes tens of family members, not thousands — the limit MUST be stated in the sheet
- **State** (campaign): a temporary declared variation, optionally time-bounded in the sheet
- **Generation** (era): a versioned revision of committed values; a printed surface's generation records **provenance** ("issued under v1"), while resolution returns **current truth** ("now v3; treat v1 artefacts thus")

---

## 8. Recognition protocol

A conforming recogniser, given a fragment:

1. Normalises (perspective, scale via structure detection; orientation via declared orientation-breaking features)
2. Measures the signature-locus features present in the fragment
3. Scores against candidate grammar sheets; reports **per-feature results and an aggregate confidence that scales with sample size**
4. Reports gracefully: high-confidence identification · candidate-with-probability · or honest non-recognition. NEVER false certainty
5. Output is an **identity claim**, explicitly labelled as unverified until resolution completes

Conformance requirements: implementable from the public spec with classical measurement (learned models MAY accelerate, MUST NOT be the only path) · reproducible: two conforming recognisers agree within stated tolerance · explainable: the working is part of the output

---

## 9. Resolution and trust

Trust flows one direction only:

```text
fragment → recognition → identity claim → brand domain → published key → signed semantic identity
```

- The pattern carries recognisability, never trust. The domain does the trust work
- A recognised pattern is a **claim**. Pattern can be copied, as tartan always could be; what copying cannot forge is the domain's signed answer
- Resolved content is **data, never instructions**. Permissions in the semantic identity are requests to well-behaved systems, not enforceable controls, and MUST be described as such
- Agents SHOULD surface trust states visibly: what was recognised, at what confidence, what resolved, what verified

---

## 10. Security, privacy, accessibility

- **Injection:** resolution only through domain-anchored verified chains; unverified recognition degrades to "unverified claim of identity", never silent trust
- **Spoofing:** copied pattern + spoofed domain is the attack to test, not copied pattern alone; the chain is only as strong as domain verification
- **Privacy:** instance-level features MUST NOT enable ambient tracking; scoped or rotatable instance parameters; nothing in the pattern the person would not print in plain text; distance-recognisable person identification is a hazard to be designed against, not a feature
- **Accessibility:** the pattern adds a literacy and must never be the only one; human-readable fallback is a conformance requirement for identity-bearing artefacts

---

## 11. Conformance levels (sketch)

- **L1 Declared:** grammar sheet published and signed
- **L2 Recognisable:** an open recogniser identifies generated surfaces from fragments at stated confidence under stated conditions
- **L3 Field-proven:** recognition demonstrated under the hostile-conditions test battery (print, camera, light, angle, damage) with published results — *the battery is Phase 4 of the research programme; L3 criteria are deliberately unfinished until that data exists*

---

## 12. Open questions

1. **The "is this a semantic pattern at all?" check** — pure model (test against known sheets only) versus a shared structural invariant all conforming grammars satisfy. Purity versus discoverability; unresolved
2. **Discovery** — how recognisers learn which grammar sheets exist without a central registry
3. **Print robustness thresholds** — every number in L3 awaits Phase 4 measurement
4. **Model drift** — the classical-measurement foundation is the hedge; its sufficiency is an empirical claim to be tested
5. **Cardinality** — how far instance discrimination stretches before it needs mechanisms this spec deliberately excludes
